# The 15-Minute Business Sale Readiness Check

For New Zealand business owners thinking about selling in the next 6 to 24 months.

Use this checklist to get a practical sense of how ready your business may be for sale, where buyers are likely to focus, and what may need work before you go to market.

## How to use it

Go through each section and mark:
- `Yes` if it is in good shape
- `Needs work` if it is partly there or inconsistent
- `No` if it is missing or unclear

This is not a formal valuation. It is a practical readiness check to help you spot strengths, risks, and next steps.

---

## 1. Owner Readiness

Before a sale process starts, buyers and brokers usually need clarity on your timing and goals.

- Do you know why you want to sell?
- Do you have a rough timeframe for when you would like to exit?
- Do you know what you would want from a sale, apart from price?
- Have you thought about whether you want to stay on for a handover period?
- Are business partners or key family members aligned on the plan?

**If this section needs work:**
Unclear goals can slow decisions, create mixed signals for buyers, and make negotiations harder.

---

## 2. Financial Readiness

Clean, understandable numbers are one of the first things serious buyers look for.

- Are your financial statements up to date?
- Are your sales, costs, and margins reasonably easy to explain?
- Can you clearly separate personal expenses from business expenses?
- Do you have a good picture of normal owner earnings?
- Are there any unusual one-off costs or income items that need explaining?
- Are GST, PAYE, and other tax obligations up to date?

**If this section needs work:**
Messy or unclear financials can reduce buyer confidence and weaken offers.

---

## 3. Operational Readiness

Buyers want a business that can keep running without everything depending on the owner.

- Are the main day-to-day processes documented or easy to explain?
- Can staff handle most operations without you being involved in every decision?
- Are key supplier and customer relationships stable?
- Is the business overly reliant on you personally?
- Are there recurring operational issues that could concern a buyer?

**If this section needs work:**
Heavy owner dependence often makes a business harder to sell and can reduce perceived value.

---

## 4. Customer and Revenue Quality

Not all revenue is viewed the same way. Buyers want to understand how stable and repeatable it is.

- Do you know where your leads or customers mainly come from?
- Is revenue spread across multiple customers rather than concentrated in one or two?
- Do you have repeat customers or recurring work?
- Can you explain recent sales trends clearly?
- Are there any major customer losses or revenue dips that need context?

**If this section needs work:**
Revenue concentration or inconsistency can make buyers nervous, even if turnover looks strong.

---

## 5. Team and People Risk

Buyers often ask whether the business relies on one or two key people.

- Do staff have clear roles and responsibilities?
- Are employment arrangements documented properly?
- Would the business still run if a key staff member left?
- Is there anyone whose departure would seriously affect revenue or operations?
- Are there known staff issues that should be resolved before sale?

**If this section needs work:**
People risk can create uncertainty around continuity after settlement.

---

## 6. Legal and Compliance Basics

You do not need to have everything perfect, but basic compliance matters.

- Are key contracts available and current?
- Are leases, licences, and permits current where relevant?
- Are there any disputes, claims, or legal issues that may need to be disclosed?
- Is your business structure clear and documented?
- Are there any compliance matters that a buyer would need to know about?

**If this section needs work:**
Gaps here do not always stop a sale, but they can create delays, price pressure, or extra conditions.

---

## 7. Presentation to Market

Good businesses can still underperform in market if the story is unclear.

- Can you explain simply what the business does and who it serves?
- Do you know the main strengths a buyer would value?
- Can you explain the growth opportunities realistically?
- Are the weaker areas understood and manageable?
- Is the business being presented honestly and clearly, without overselling?

**If this section needs work:**
Poor presentation can reduce enquiry quality and make a business look riskier than it is.

---

## 8. Timing Check

Sometimes the right advice is to prepare first, not rush to market.

- Is the business trading steadily enough to start a sale process?
- Would waiting 6 to 12 months likely improve value or buyer confidence?
- Are there simple improvements that could make the business more attractive before listing?
- Are there any urgent issues that should be fixed first?

**If this section needs work:**
Better timing can materially improve buyer interest, negotiation position, and sale smoothness.

---

## Quick Scorecard

Count how many times you marked `Yes`, `Needs work`, and `No`.

### Mostly `Yes`
You may be in a solid position to start a conversation about sale strategy, likely buyer fit, and market readiness.

### A mix of `Yes` and `Needs work`
You may be closer than you think, but a few targeted improvements could make the process smoother and strengthen buyer confidence.

### Several `No` answers
That does not mean you cannot sell. It usually means preparation will matter. The good news is that many issues can be improved well before going to market.
