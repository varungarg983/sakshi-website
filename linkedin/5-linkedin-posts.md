## Post 1

If you are thinking about selling your business, start earlier than you think.

One of the most common mistakes I see is owners waiting until they are ready to exit before they start preparing.

In most cases, the better sale processes start well before the business goes to market.

That gives you time to:

- tidy the numbers
- reduce owner dependence
- fix small issues buyers may question
- present the business more clearly

Preparation does not just make the process smoother.

It can improve the quality of buyer interest as well.

## Post 2

Buying a business is not just about liking the idea of it.

A business can sound great on the surface and still carry more risk than expected.

Before getting too excited, I think buyers should get clear on a few things:

- where the revenue is coming from
- how dependent the business is on the current owner
- whether margins are sustainable
- whether key staff or customers create concentration risk

The headline numbers matter.

But the story behind those numbers matters just as much.

If you are exploring business opportunities in New Zealand, I am always happy to have a straightforward conversation.

## Post 3

Not every business is ready to go to market straight away.

That is not bad news.

It usually just means the next step is preparation, not pressure.

Sometimes a few practical improvements can make a real difference:

- cleaner financials
- clearer processes
- less reliance on the owner
- better explanation of recent performance

A business does not have to be perfect.

But it does help to understand what a serious buyer is likely to focus on.

If you are unsure where your business stands, that is exactly the kind of conversation worth having early.

## Post 4

One thing I have learnt in business sales is that people usually want clarity more than complexity.

Buying or selling a business can feel overwhelming if every conversation is full of jargon.

Most people just want to understand:

- where they stand
- what happens next
- what the risks are
- what they should do first

That is why I prefer a practical and straightforward approach.

Clear advice helps people make better decisions.

If you are exploring a sale or a purchase, I am happy to start with a simple conversation.

## Post 5

One question sellers should ask themselves before going to market is:

What will a buyer question first?

In many cases, it is not the thing the owner expects.

It could be:

- messy numbers
- owner dependence
- customer concentration
- weak documentation
- unexplained changes in performance

Looking at your business through a buyer’s eyes can be incredibly useful.

It helps you prepare better, present the business better, and handle buyer questions with more confidence.
